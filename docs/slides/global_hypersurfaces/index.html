Codazzi Equation for Weingarten

Codazzi Equation for Weingarten

\[ \nabla_X W(Y) = \nabla_Y W (X) \]

Follows from \(\nabla_X A (Y, Z) = \nabla_Y A (X, Z)\) and \(\nabla g = 0\)

Codazzi Equation for Weingarten

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} D_X [W(Y)] &= -D_X [D_Y N] \\ &= -D_Y [D_X N] - D_{[X, Y]} N \\ &\quad - \Rm^{\RR^n}(X, Y) N \\ &= D_Y[W(X)] + W([X, Y]) \end{split} \end{equation*}

Codazzi Equation for Weingarten

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \nabla_X W(Y) &= \nabla_X [W(Y)] - W(\nabla_X Y) \\ &= D_X [W(Y)] + A(X, W(Y))N - W(\nabla_X Y) \\ &= D_Y [W(X)] + A(Y, W(X))N \\ &\quad - W(\nabla_X Y - [X, Y]) \\ &= D_Y [W(X)] + A(Y, W(X))N - W(\nabla_Y X) \\ &= \nabla_Y W(X) \end{split} \end{equation*}

Umbilicity Theorem

Umbilicity Theorem

Umbilic Points

A point \(p \in M\) is umbilic if \(\kappa_1 (p) = \dots = \kappa_n(p)\). That is if \(W_p = \kappa \Id\).

Umbilicity Theorem

If every point of a connected hypersurface \(M\) is umbilic then either \(M\) lies in a hyperplane or a sphere.

Umbilicity Lemma

Umbilicity Lemma

Umbilicity Lemma

If \(W = \kappa \Id\) for a smooth function \(\kappa : M \to \RR\), then \(\kappa \equiv \text{ const}\).

Umbilicity Lemma

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \nabla_X W(Y) &= \nabla_X [W(Y)] - W(\nabla_X Y) \\ &= \nabla_X (\kappa Y) - \kappa \nabla_X Y \\ &= (\partial_X \kappa) Y \end{split} \end{equation*}

Then for all \(X, Y\) \[ (\partial_X \kappa) Y = \nabla_X W(Y) = \nabla_Y W(X) = (\partial_Y \kappa) X \]

Hence \(d\kappa \equiv 0\)

Umbilicity Theorem: Plane

Umbilicity Theorem: Plane

Umbilicity Theorem: Plane

If \(W \equiv 0\) on a connected open set \(U \subseteq M\), then \(U\) lies in a hyperplane.

Umbilicity Theorem: Plane

Let \(u_0, u \in U\) and \(\gamma(t) \in U\) with \(\gamma(0) = u_0\), \(\gamma(1) = u\)

\[ \partial_t [N(\gamma(t))] = dN(\gamma') = -W(\gamma') = 0 \]

\[ N(\gamma(t)) \equiv N_0 \]

Umbilicity Theorem: Plane

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \ip{\gamma(0) - u_0}{N_0} &= \ip{u_0 - u_)}{N_0} = 0 \\ \partial_t \ip{\gamma(t) - u_0}{N_0} &= \ip{\gamma'(t)}{N(\gamma(t))} = 0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}

\[ \ip{\gamma(t) - u_0}{N_0} \equiv 0 \]

\[ \ip{u - u_0}{N_0} = \ip{\gamma(1) - u_0}{N_0} = 0 \]

Every \(u \in U\) is in the plane through \(u_0\) perpendicular to \(N_0\)

Umbilicity Theorem: Sphere

Umbilicity Theorem: Sphere

Umbilicity Theorem: Sphere

If \(W \equiv \kappa \neq 0\) on a connected open set \(U \subseteq M\), then \(U\) lies in a sphere of radius \(1/\kappa\)

Note if \(\kappa < 0\), changing \(N\) to \(-N\) gives \(\kappa > 0\)

Umbilicity Theorem: Sphere

Let \(u_0, u \in U\) and \(\gamma(t) \in U\) with \(\gamma(0) = u_0\), \(\gamma(1) = u_0\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \partial_t \left[\gamma(t) + \tfrac{1}{\kappa} N(\gamma(t))\right] &= \gamma' + \frac{1}{\kappa} dN(\gamma') \\ &= \gamma' - \frac{1}{\kappa} W(\gamma') \\ &= \gamma' - \frac{1}{\kappa} \kappa \gamma' \\ &= 0 \end{split} \end{equation*}

Umbilicity Theorem: Sphere

\[ \gamma(t) + \frac{1}{\kappa} N(\gamma(t)) \equiv p_0 = u_0 + \frac{1}{\kappa} N(u_0) \]

\[ \abs{\gamma(t) - p_0} = \abs{-\frac{1}{\kappa} N} = \abs{\frac{1}{\kappa}} \] Thus every \(u = \gamma(1) \in \SS^n_{1/\kappa} (p_0)\)

Isometries

Isometries

Isometries

A map \(\varphi : U_1 \subseteq (M_1, g_1) \to U_2 \subseteq (M_2, g_2)\) is a (local) isometry if \[ g_2(d\varphi(X), d\varphi(Y)) = g_1(X, Y) \]

Plane to Cylinder

\[ \varphi(u, v) = (\cos u, \sin u, v) \] is an isometry from the plane to the cylinder

\[ g_{\text{Cylinder}} = \Id \] in the basis \(\partial_u \varphi, \partial_v \varphi\)

Intrinsic Geometry

Intrinsic Geometry

Intrinsic Geometry

A “quantity” is intrinsic if it is invariant under isometry (and hence depends only on the geometry determined by the metric \(g\))

Biliner form: \[ B_{\varphi(p)} (d\varphi_p(X(p)), d\varphi_p(Y(p))) = B_p(X(p), Y(p)) \]

Linear map: \[ T_{\varphi(p)} (d\varphi_p (X_p)) = d\varphi_p(T_p(X(p))) \]

Connection and Riemann Curvature are Intrinsic

Connection and Riemann Curvature are Intrinsic

Levi-Civita Connection

Show that \(\nabla\) is uniquely determined by

\begin{align*} \nabla_X Y - \nabla_Y X = [X, Y], & \text{torision free} \\ \partial_X [g(Y, Z)] = g(\nabla_X Y, Z) + g(Y, \nabla_X Z), & \text{metric compatible} \\ \end{align*}

Levi-Civita Connection

The Levi-Civita connection is the unique torsion free, metric compatible connection determined by the previous exercise. It’s determined uniquely by the Koszul formula

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} 2 g(\nabla_X Y, Z) &= \partial_X [g(Y, Z)] + \partial_Y [g(X, Z)] - \partial_Z [g(X, Y)] \\ &\quad + g([X, Y], Z) - g([Y, Z], X) + g([Z, X], Y) \end{split} \end{equation*}

Connection is Intrinsic

Let \(\varphi : U \to V\) be an isometry. Then \[ d\varphi_p(\nabla_X Y (p)) = \nabla_{d\varphi_p(X)} d\varphi_p (Y) \]

Follows from the Koszul formula, invariance of \(g\) under isometries, and the naturality of the Lie bracket and differentiation (chain rule).

Curvature Tensor is Intrinsic

\(\Rm\) is intrinsic

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} d\varphi[\Rm(X, Y) Z] &= d\varphi\big[\nabla_X (\nabla_Y Z) - \nabla_Y (\nabla_X Z) - \nabla_{[X, Y]} Z\big] \\ &= \nabla_{d\varphi X} (d\varphi(\nabla_Y Z)) - \nabla_{d\varphi X} (d\varphi(\nabla_Y Z)) \\ &\quad - \nabla_{d\varphi ([X, Y])} d\varphi Z \\ &= \nabla_{d\varphi X} (\nabla_{d\varphi Y} d\varphi Z) - \nabla_{d\varphi Y} (\nabla_{d\varphi Y} d\varphi Z) \\ &\quad - \nabla_{[d\varphi X, d\varphi Y]} d\varphi Z \\ &=\Rm(d\varphi(X), d\varphi(Y)) d\varphi(Z) \end{split} \end{equation*}

Gauss Theorema Egregium

Gauss Theorema Egregium

Remarkable Theorem

Gauss Theorema Egregium

The Gauss curvature of a surface is intrinsic. That is, if \((S_1, g_1)\) and \((S_2, g_2)\) are locally isometric, then \(K_1 = K_2\).

Gauss Theorema Egregium

Diagonalising \(W(e_i) = \kappa_i e_i\) and using the Gauss equation

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \Rm(e_1, e_2, e_2, e_1) &= A(e_2, e_2) A(e_1, e_1) - A(e_1, e_2)^2 \\ &= \kappa_1 \kappa_2 = K \end{split} \end{equation*}

Remarks on Gauss Theorema Egregium

Remarks on Gauss Theorema Egregium

Non-isometric Surfaces

These surfaces are not locally isometric

  • Sphere: \(K \equiv 1\)
  • Torus: \(K\) non-constant but changing sign
  • Cylinder: \(K \equiv 0\)
  • Paraboloid: \(K\) non-constant and positive

Note all surfaces are locally diffeomorphic via local parametrisations!

Corrugation

Source: The Perfect Slum: Corrugated Steel

Corrugation

  • Folding a sheet of (paper, metal, cardboard) along a line introduces curvature but does not change the geometry provided no stretching occurs.
  • Thus one principal curvature is non-zero, but Gauss’ theorem forces the other to vanish since \[ 0 \underset{\text{Gauss Theorem}}{=} K = \kappa_1 \kappa_2 \]
  • Introduces rigidity in one direction and flexibility in the other.

Map Making
