Commuting Vector Fields

Commuting Vector Fields

Lie Bracket

Given two vector fields \(X, Y\) there is a unique vector field \(Z\) such that for all smooth functions \(f\), \[ \partial_Z f := \partial_X \partial_Y f - \partial_Y \partial_X f \] We write \([X, Y]\) for \(Z\), called the commutator or Lie Bracket of \(X\) and \(Y\).

Lie Bracket

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \partial_X \partial_Y f &= \partial_X (Y^j \partial_j f) \\ &= X^i \partial_i (Y^j \partial_j f) \\ &= X^i \partial_i Y^j \partial_j f + X^i Y^j \partial^2_{ij} f \\ \partial_Y \partial_X f &= Y^i \partial_i X^j \partial_j f + Y^i X^j \partial^2_{ij} f \end{split} \end{equation*}

\[ Z^j = X^i \partial_i Y^j - Y^i \partial_i X^j \]

Lie Bracket

\[ X = (x, 0), \quad Y = (y^2, xy), \quad [X, Y] = (-y^2, xy) \]

\[ X = (x, 0), \quad Y = (xy, y^2), \quad [X, Y] = (0, 0) \]

Naturality of Lie Bracket

\[ d\tau([X, Y]) = [d\tau(X), d\tau(Y)] \]

\[ [\bar{X}, \bar{Y}]|_S = [X, Y] \]

Naturality of Lie Bracket

Let \(Z = [X, Y]\). Then

\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \partial_{d\tau(Z)} f &= d\tau_j^k Z^j \partial_k f \\ &= d\tau_j^k (X^i \partial_i Y^j - Y^i \partial_i X^j) \partial_k f \end{split} \end{equation*}

Second Fundamental Form

Second Fundamental Form

Let \(S\) be a regular surface with metric \(g\), connection \(\nabla\). The second fundamental form is the field of bilinear forms, \[ A(X, Y) = \ip{D_{\bar{X}} \bar{Y}}{N} \] where \(X, Y\) are tangent vector fields and \(\bar{X}, \bar{Y}\) are any smooth local extensions of \(X, Y\).

Gauss Equation

We will write \(D_X Y\) instead of \(D_{\bar{X} \bar{Y}\) with the understanding that \(D_X Y\) is well defined independently of the extensions \(\bar{X}, \bar{Y}\).

\[ D_X Y = \nabla_X Y + A(X, Y) N \]

Follows from \(T\RR^3 \simeq TS \oplus NS\)

\begin{align*} \nabla_X Y &= \pi_{TS} (D_X Y) \\ \pi_N (D_X Y) &= A(X, Y) N \end{align*}

Example: Sphere

For the sphere, \[ A = g. \]

Example: Graph

For \(S = \graph(f)\) \[ A = . \]

Symmetry and Weingarten Shape Operator

Symmetry and Weingarten Shape Operator

Symmetry

\[ A(X, Y) = A(Y, X) \]

Follows from \([\bar{X}, \bar{Y}] = [X, Y]\) is tangent.

Weingarten Shape Operator

\[ \mathcal{W}(X) = -D_{\bar{X}} N \]

Weingarten and SFF

\[ A(X, Y) = g(\mathcal{W}(X), Y) = g(X, \mathcal{Y}(X)) \]

Principal Curvatures

Principal Curvatures

The principal curvatures \(S\) are the eigenvalues \(\kappa_1, \kappa_2\) of \(\mathcal{W}\). By convention we order them as \(\kappa_1 \leq \kappa_2\).

Note that \(\mathcal{W}\) is self-adjoint, hence is diagonalizable.

Gauss and Mean Curvatures

The Mean Curvature is \[ H = \kappa_1 + \kappa_2 = 2 \Tr(\mathcal{W}) \] and the Gauss Curvature is \[ K = \kappa_1 \kappa_2 = \det \mathcal{W}. \]

Examples

: Plane

\(\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = 0 \Rightarrow H = K = 0\)

: Sphere

\(\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = 1 \Rightarrow H = K = 1\)

: Cylinder

\(\kappa_1 = 0, \kappa_2 = 1 \Rightarrow H = 1, \quad K = 0\)
