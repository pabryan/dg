Orientation of Vector Spaces

An orientation on an \(n\)-dimensional vector space \(V\) is an equivalence class of ordered bases \(\mathcal{E} = (e_1, \dots, e_n)\) where \(\mathcal{E} \sim \mathcal{F}\) if the change of basis matrix \(A_{\mathcal{E}\mathcal{F}}\) has positive determinant.

Since \(\det \left(A_{\mathcal{E}\mathcal{F}} A_{\mathcal{F}\mathcal{G}}\right) = \det \left(A_{\mathcal{E}\mathcal{F}}\right) \det\left(A_{\mathcal{F}\mathcal{G}}\right)\), we do indeed have an equivalence relation, and there are precisely two equivalence classes.

Orientation Examples

Compute the change of basis and orientation from \(\mathcal{E} = (e_1, e_2)\) to \((e_1, e_1 + e_2), \quad (e_1, -e_2), \quad (e_2, e_1).\)

Right hand orientation: \((e_1, e_2, e_3), (e_1, e_3, -e_2), \dots\)

Left hand orientation: \((e_2, e_1, e_3), (e_1, -e_2, e_3), \dots\)

Orientation Preserving Maps

An invertible linear map \(T : V \to V\) is orientation preserving if \(T(\mathcal{O}) = \mathcal{O}\) for both orientations \(\mathcal{O}\). That is, if \[ \det \begin{pmatrix} T(e_1), \cdots, T(e_n) \end{pmatrix} = \det \begin{pmatrix} e_1, \cdots, e_n \end{pmatrix} \] or equivalently if \(\det T > 0\).

Orientation Preserving Examples

#+BEGINenv eg \[ T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad T = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \]

Orientation Reversing Examples

\[ T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad T = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad T = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. \] #+ENDenv

Orientation of Surfaces

Orientation of the tangent plane

Local parametrisation: \(\varphi : U \to S\). \[ \left(\frac{\partial \varphi}{\partial u}, \frac{\partial \varphi}{\partial v}\right), \quad \left(\frac{\partial \varphi}{\partial v}, \frac{\partial \varphi}{\partial u}\right) \]

Surface Orientation

#+BEIGNenv defn A regular surface, \(S\) is orientable if there is a cover \(\varphi_{\alpha} : U_{\alpha} \to S\) such that \(\det(\tau_{\alpha\beta}) > 0\) for all \(\alpha, \beta\). #+ENDenv

That is, the orientation induced by \(\varphi_{\alpha}\) is the same as the orientation induced by \(\varphi_{\beta}\).

Orientation and Normal

A surface \(S\) is orientable if and only if there is a differentiable field, \(N\) of unit normal vectors. That is, if and only there exists a differentiable map \(N : S \to \RR^3\) such that \(\abs{N(x)} = 1\) for all \(x \in S\) and such that \(N(x) \perp X\) for all \(X \in T_x S\).

Two Orientations - Two Unit Normal Fields

Remember there are precisely two orientations!

There are two possible unit normal fields, \(N\) and \(-N\). Choosing an orientation is equivalent to choosing a normal field.

Orientation and Normal Proof

The proof of the theorem follows from the following lemma:

#+BEGINenv lem Let \(\varphi (u, v) : U \subseteq \RR^2 \to S\) and \(\psi (s, t) : V \subseteq \RR^2 \to S\) be local parametrisations. Then \[ \partial_u \varphi \times \partial_v \varphi = \left[\det d(\psi^{-1} \circ \varphi)\right] \partial_s \psi \times \partial_t \psi. \] #+ENDenv lem

Gauss Map

An orientable surface \(S\) along with a choice of orientation is called an oriented surface.

Let \(S\) be an oriented surface. The Gauss Map is the unit normal map \[ x \in S \mapsto N(x) \in \sphere^2 = \{X \in \RR^3 : \|X\| = 1\}. \]

Gauss Map in Local Coordinates

With respect to a local parametrisation \[ N = \frac{\partial_u \varphi \times \partial_v \varphi}{\abs{\partial_u \varphi \times \partial_v \varphi}}. \]

Examples

\[ S = \{x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1\}, \quad N(p) = p \]

\[ S = \{(x, y, f(x, y))\}, \quad N(x, y, f(x)) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + f_x^2 + f_y^2}} (-f_x, -f_y, 1). \]

\[ S = \{F^{-1}(c)\}, \quad N(p) = \frac{\nabla F(p)}{|\nabla F(p)|}. \]
